Early Civilization

PUBLIC DOCUMENT ID: ARCH-2425-EC01 PUBLISHED: 2425.03.12 LAST UPDATED: 2428.11.05

Archival Preamble: On the Contested Nature of the Early Record

The reconstruction of the Age of Convergence remains subject to scrutiny. Unlike the later 16th-century era, defined by the Theocratic Regime's systematic erasure, Early Civilization suffers from a scarcity of unified records. Archaeological analysis suggests a vibrant planetary society, yet much of our knowledge is derived from fragmentary merchant logs and residual gravitational markers.

Our findings indicate a plurality of divergent evolutions—often unified by trade necessities and a shared, dangerous reverence for Non-Baryonic Matter.

This document synthesizes the most credible sources available, but researchers are encouraged to approach conclusions with caution, acknowledging the potential for revision as new evidence emerges.

Note: Unlike other records, The timeline laid out here is for the convenience of the reader and researchers, more detailed reports are available in the Research sections of the archive. It is unlikely that everything preceding the regime can be classified as a single age, in fact it is more than likely that the Age of Convergence was a series of distinct eras. Current historical research is ongoing and has not yet found a clear indication, or consensus, on the proper structure of pre-regime history.

"There are no other words, but void, utter void. The contempt the regime held for history, for science, for information, it is unimaginable in our modern society. The loss of information is immeasurable; we don't even know where to start to figure out how much, we've lost."
~ excerpted from a lecture by Dr. Liora Venn, Member of the Historical Comittee on Pre-Regime Studies

The Age of Convergence

Following the Prehistoric Era, Early Civilization marks the first recorded efforts to unify disparate societal clusters. Spanning the 9th to the late 15th century, this era was defined by the expansion of continental trade routes and the emergence of formalized knowledge-sharing networks.

While the presence of stable Non-Baryonic Matter pockets influenced settlement patterns, the backbone of civilization remained anchored in traditional logistics. Maritime routes across the Shimmering Expanse facilitated the exchange of bioluminescent pigments and deep-sea mineral salts, while overland caravans established the first reliable postal relay systems using native pack-beasts.

"The pulse of our world is found in the wake of the trade-ships and the dust of the caravans, not just the humming of the deep stone."
— attributed to Archon Valerius, circa 1142.

Early Research & Scholasticism

Beyond the study of 'The Essence', early institutions focused heavily on Celestial Pathfinding and Biological Taxonomy. The Grand Conservatory of Aethelgard, though situated near an NBM crater, something that no doubt is partially responsible for the prestige of the Conservatory. There is no evidence to suggest it focused its studies on NBM specifically. The Conservatory still exists today and is known as the University of Aethelgard, when exactly it evolved into a modern scientific institution is unclear.

Based on the case of the Grand Conservatory of Aethelgard it is assumed that most universities and research institutes, not founded under the regime, track their origin back to the Age of Convergance.

Scholastic efforts in this period were often decentralized, occurring within guild-halls and temple-archives. Researchers developed sophisticated methods of Linguistic Forensics to decode the few surviving etchings from the prehistoric past, attempting to bridge the gap between their present and the 'First Ancestors' described in folklore.

The Role of Non-Baryonic Matter

NBM was identified through its gravitational effects and faint bioluminescence. Every nation-state preceding the theocratic regime held deep reverence for it, though the expression was divergent: NBM was worshipped as "Cooling Spirits" in the south and "Frozen Starlight" in the north.

Manipulation was primitive and lethal. Without containment, Non-Baryonic-Baryon Reactions led to localized spatial collapses. These sites often became the foundation for Resonance Architecture—structures built to harmonize with the local gravitational flux.

Gravimetric Accounting

While most commerce relied on physical weight and barter, administrative control over NBM was maintained through Gravimetric Accounting. Bureaucrats and Researchers utilized handheld devices consisting of metallic weights suspended in a calibrated frame. These instruments were both used to gather accurate readings of NBM 'purity' for taxation and trade, and for the purposes of scientific study.

Economic Disparities & Rent-Seeking

The Age of Convergence, despite its name, was marked by deepening systemic inequities. Early economic structures were increasingly dominated by Rent-Seeking Behavior—a phenomenon where local elites extracted wealth without contributing to production, primarily through the control of geographic and infrastructural bottlenecks.

Land-holding clans in the Iron Foothills and maritime republics in the Sunken Spires established complex systems of passage fees, storage tariffs, and "stability tithes." These were not merely taxes but aggressive monopolies on resource access. In rural clusters, this manifested as Land-Rent Usury, where agrarian populations were forced into perpetual debt to city-based owners who held ancestral claims to fertile soil or water-table access points.

These disparities were exacerbated by, but not limited to, the NBM trade. Archive fragments from the late 14th century, such as the Scrolls of the Unbalanced Scale, complain of both "gravimetric usury" and the "unearned harvest" collected by urban masters. These writings document a growing resentment toward the concentration of capital in hubs like Aethelgard, which prospered while the 'un-resonated' fringes stagnated under the weight of artificial scarcity.

Contemporary historians view these grievances as proto-revolutionary sediment. The nascent rejection of these unearned hierarchies provided the fertile societal ground that the later Theocratic Regime would eventually exploit, codifying secular greed into divine law.

Major Historical Occurrences

The Great Schism of the Mons Olympus (1314) saw city-states fight for the Aethelgard Crater, a major surface deposit. The conflict ended with the Accord of the Shimmering Veil, establishing a global standard for gravimetric measurement—the first step toward a unified planetary economy.
