Early Revolutionary Period

PUBLIC DOCUMENT ID: ARCH-HIST-2245 PUBLISHED: 2135.04.11 LAST UPDATED: 2189.10.19

Overview

The Early Revolutionary Period marks the initial phase of organized resistance against the theocratic regime that had dominated for centuries. This era, spanning approximately from Standard Year 2056 to Standard Year 2098, witnessed the formation of the first syndicalist cells and the development of revolutionary theory that would eventually form the foundation of the Stellar Syndicate.

Origins of the Movement

Industrial Awakening

The rapid industrialization of the urban regions created new social conditions that challenged the existing power structures. Workers in manufacturing centers began to organize, initially for basic labor protections, but gradually developing more radical political consciousness.

Key developments during this period included the formation of mutual aid societies, the establishment of underground educational networks, and the creation of parallel economic structures that operated outside theocratic control.

During this early period, oppression was rampant, with the regime employing harsh measures to suppress dissent, doubt, and organization.

The First Councils

In 2064, workers in the Kaelion shipyards established one of the first workers' council, which served as a model for future organizational structures. This council coordinated strike actions and provided mutual support for workers' families during periods of industrial action.

With the first broad industrial strike recorded in 16.9.2064 the Kaelion Workers Council was declared a Terrorist Organization and declared subject to heracy laws. At a Time in which the council had no recorded position on Non-baryonic Matter. There are no records or indications to the contrary.

Even though it was in constant conflict with Guardians of Faith, subject to raids, crackdowns, and arbitrary mass execution of suspected members. To a point where the average councilor lived less than 7 months after their election. It persisted as a stable entity for a decade.

The council was eventually functionally dissolved in 2075 after the Massacre known as Week of Faith, during which the entire Kaelonian province was systematically depopulated by the Theocratic Regime.

Historically, the Kaelion Workers Council is especially notable in several ways. It served as the first major distributor of The Workers Papers and provided both literary education and distribution infrastructure that was critical to the development of early revolutionary theory. It also is extensively studied in contemporary resistance theory, mainly in respect to its incredible resilience, and eventual re-establishment. Furthermore, it is the first recorded Workers Council that had synthetic members. With the poem Does this unit posess a Soul? directly attributed to a member of the Kaelion Workers Council.

The Workers Papers

The Workers Papers are best understood as a collaborative collective, intellectual writing effort. Writing and organization were facilitated via the Workers Math method, which is the origin of the today still widely used Recombinational Versioning Scheme. The Workers Papers were a collection of writings, essays, information, and pornographic material, that define early revolutionary theory and serve as the basis for the development of the Stellar Syndicate. The first recorded publication of The Workers Papers was in 1969, and was published by the Kaelion Workers Council.

Versioning: Any given edition of The Workers Papers would include a variety of articles from different sources, and about different subjects. Coherence was maintained through a system of Versioning known colloquially as Workers Math. For any given published Article in The Workers Papers there was a string of numbers the exact scheme varies, but generally it followed the format of: {DistrictNumber}:{Edition}-{ArticleNumber} For example: 1:2-3 would refer to the cannonical release from the Katherion Workers Council (District 1, by the God's Order System), It would be the second published Paper by that Council, and be the third Article in it. Since any given edition of The Workers Papers would be a combination of different articles, the Versioning system allowed multiple articles from different sources to be distributed simultaneously. In modern historical analysis, the scheme is often extended to this: {DistrictNumber}:{Edition}-{ArticleNumber}/{Containing Batch}

Batching: Batching was the process of distributing lists of Version numbers used as a form of reading list. This was a practice widely employed by readers of the Papers. Often with catchy names. A few notable examples being the various Worker's Manifesto batches, the Worker's Tools collections, the Shiny Rock Trilogy series, or the widely popular altho in historical context less important and widely differing Hear me out collections of pornographic material, remarkably often containing depictions of imaginary extra terrestrial beings.

Worker's Manifesto: The Workers Manifesto was a widely distrbuted batch of articles concerning the core principles of what later became known as syndicalism. Including anything from proposed constitutions, for various entities, some of which implemented in practice, multiple even contradictory philosophical and ethical essays. The Manifesto was the first widely read and published collection of early revolutionary writings, concerning governance specifically.

Worker's Tools: The Worker's Tools was a collection of guides, experiences, and tactics for effective organization and resistance. Including anything from warfare doctrine, to clandestine operation guidelines, to the fabrication of weaponry. Also including extensive writing on organizational principles, analysis of theocratic talking points, and how to refute them. The tools represent the first widely read and published collection of early revolutionary writings concerning organizational principles.

Shiny Rock Trilogy: A series of fictional stories, directly or indirectly, concerning doubt about the religious foundation of the Theocratic Regime. Their irreverent nature made them divisive in its time, with some council distributions only including them with a specific preamble, in an attempt to soften the critique. Reasoning behind this is today debated, it might be motivated by honest belief in the theocratic myth, or it might have been an attempt to increase popularity and reach by having a more neutral stance. Even though the name might imply otherwise there have been more than three entries in the series. Annotated and unannotated versions of the stories are widely read and discussed to date. Both amongst historians and the wider population of the stellar syndicate.

Does This Unit have a Soul?: Is a poem of unknown authorship, attributed either to a synthetic member of the Kaelion Workers Council, or a mechanic in the Kaelion Shipyard. The Poem concerns the agency and the role of synthetic beings in society. Explicitly in contrast to the Theocratic Mythology, according to which, synthetic beings do not possess a soul and are not to be treated as people. It sparked wide global discussions about the position of synthetic beings in the revolutionary effort. How important it was at the time is a matter of discussion amongst contemporary historians. However, its effects are undeniable, with the explicit inclusion of synthetic liberation in later revolutionary writing, movements, and councils. Early anti-synthetic-discrimination measures within council constitutions can be directly traced back to the influence of the poem.

Hear me out: The Hear me out collections were less a coherent batch, and more a collection of popular pornographic material. Often highly personalized to individuals or tailored to specific sexual interests. These collections are historically notable for three distinct reasons. Representing the first widely recorded structural push towards sexual exploration and sexual liberation, in strict defiance of the Theocratic sexual laws and norms. Today it is also often argued that a significant amount of the Papers' early popularity was stemming from the deliberately allowed inclusion of pornographic material. The statistically significant amount of pornographic material regarding at the time 'absurd' concepts like sexual or romantic interactions with aliens, has been widely studied in contemporary sociology.

Theoretical Development

Revolutionary theorists during this period synthesized various strands of political thought, adapting traditional syndicalist principles revolutionary thought, and essays, sourced from The Worker's Papers into coherent universal practices. Key concepts developed included:

Distributed Governance: Recognition that centralized authority was both impractical and undesirable across multiple regions. Revolutionary theory emphasized federated structures with local autonomy.

Economic Democracy: Workers' control of production was articulated not merely as ownership, but as democratic participation in all economic decisions affecting their lives and labor.

Intersystem Solidarity: Development of mechanisms for coordination and mutual aid across vast distances, laying groundwork for future stellar confederation.

Universality of Freedom: Freedom not as a privilege, but as the core attribute of any sentient being. Explicitly including synthetic lifeforms.

Challenges and Repression

The theocratic regime responded to revolutionary activity with increasing violence. Records from this period document widespread persecution of suspected revolutionaries, suppression of workers' organizations, and attempts to infiltrate and disrupt revolutionary networks.

Week of Faith

Date: 2075.12.10-1975.12.17
Location: Across the Planet
Description: The Week of faith was the to date most violent massacre comitted by the Theocratic Regime. It consisted of the complete depopulation and later destruction of major urban hubs, known centers of revolutionary activities.
Methodology: The theocratic regime deployed a combination of Non-Baryonic Warheads, satellite-facilitated bombardment, mass incarceration, torture by means of Non-Baryonic Baryon Reaction and subsequent execution by the same means.
Victims: The Massacre was conducted against the population indiscriminatly, without regard for age, religions, or revolutionary affiliation. Internal theocratic regime reports later revealed an estimate that only 1% of the victims had any revolutionary affiliation. The exact number of victims is to date debated. However, death tolls are generally accepted to be in the range of 90 to 100 million representing more than 1% of the global population at the time. It is estimated that the surface destruction caused by the deployment of Non-Baryonic-Warheads alone accounted for 5% of the planetary surface.
Outcome: The Week of Faith had a profound chilling effect on revolutionary activities. Many workers' councils were dismantled, and revolutionary networks severely disrupted. However, it also galvanized resistance to a massive degree, argubly expanding revolutionary momentum afterward. Radicalizing responses and leading to rapid militarization of the revolutionary movement.
Repercussions: After the fall of the theocratic regime, during the Revolutionary Justice Council more than 20,000 officials, researchers, soldiers, and Guardians of Faith were executed in direct connection to the Week of Faith

Continued Resistance

Despite severe repression, the movement continued to grow, sustained by deep-rooted grievances and the practical failures of the theocratic system to address the needs of the majority. The resilience demonstrated during this period would prove crucial in the subsequent phase of more direct revolutionary action.

Legacy

The Early Revolutionary Period established the organizational foundations and theoretical frameworks that would guide the revolutionary movement through its later, more militant phases. The emphasis on democratic structures, workers' control, and intersystem solidarity remains central to the Stellar Syndicate's political culture today.
