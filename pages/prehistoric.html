Prehistoric Era

PUBLIC DOCUMENT ID: ARCH-2425-PH01 PUBLISHED: 2425.02.15 LAST UPDATED: 2428.10.20

Archival Note: The Paradox of Prehistoric Clarity

It is a recurring irony in our field that the Prehistoric Era—an age separated from us by millennia of unrecorded time—often presents a clearer archaeological picture than the centuries immediately preceding the Great Revolution. Where the Theocratic Regime employed systematic archival purging to rewrite the 16th and 17th centuries, the prehistoric record was protected by its own obscurity.

"We find it easier to reconstruct the migratory patterns of a tribal cluster from ten thousand years ago than to verify the tax records of a city-state from five hundred years ago. The soil does not lie; the Theocrats, however, were masters of the written falsehood."
— Dr. Kaelen Voss, Historical Research Council, 2426.

Nature of the First Ancestors

Before the eventual sedentary shifts and the dawn of formalized cooperation, the species existed in a state of dynamic equilibrium with the planetary environment. These early hunter-gatherer groups were not merely survivors, but sophisticated navigators of a world where resource distribution was dictated by seasonal shifts and the gravitational patterns of the crust.

Nomadic mobility was the primary strategy for resilience. Clusters tracked established migration routes and timed their harvests of bio-phosphorescent flora to the lunar cycles. Social bonds were maintained through complex oral traditions and the sharing of high-energy mineral salts, which served as the biological foundation for the species' early cognitive development. This era of deep time established the instinctual drive for collective survival that remains a cornerstone of the modern syndicate.

The Evolution of Material Mastery

A defining characteristic of this era was the development of complex technology long before the establishment of permanent settlements. Unlike many other recorded civilizations where technological booms followed the neolithic shift, the ancestors achieved significant material mastery while remaining entirely nomadic.

Lithic Innovation

The earliest toolsets consisted of pressure-flaked stone derived from silicate-heavy deep-crust deposits. These were not merely sharp edges, but specialized instruments for the precise dissection of mineral-rich flora and the preparation of hides. Archaeological sites show a progression from simple core-tools to highly refined microliths—small, interchangeable blades that could be set into bone or wooden handles, allowing for easy repair during transit.

The Discovery of Primitive Forging

Perhaps the most significant deviation from traditional prehistoric models was the early mastery of thermal processing. Using natural thermal vents and controlled surface fires, nomadic clusters began to experiment with low-temperature forging. By hammering native copper and 'star-iron' (meteoric deposits), they produced durable spear-tips and utility blades that far outclassed their stone counterparts.

This early metallurgy was conducted at mobile camp-sites, using portable clay bellows and stone anvils. This 'fire-mastery' allowed the ancestors to adapt to harsher environments, securing their survival across diverse climates without sacrificing their migratory lifestyle.

The Dawn of Social Cooperation

The Prehistoric Era is characterized by the long-term stability of Hunter-Gatherer Societies. Archaeological evidence from across the primary landmasses suggests that these early societal clusters were remarkably consistent in their organization. Unlike the rigid hierarchies that would later define the planetary landscape, prehistoric life was rooted in a horizontal social structure.

Egalitarian Tribal Structures

Analyses of communal burial sites and domestic refuse patterns indicate a relatively egalitarian society. This parity was not merely resource-based but extended to the fundamental social roles within the tribe. There is no evidence of the gender-based stratification or specialized 'priest-classes' that the later regime claimed were 'divinely natural.'

Labor was divided by necessity and capability rather than birthright. Material resources were held in common stewardship; the concept of land ownership—the precursor to the rent-seeking behaviors of the Age of Convergence—simply did not exist. The 'First Ancestors' lived in fluid groups, where the success of the individual was inextricably linked to the resilience of the collective.

NBM in the Prehistoric Mind

While Non-Baryonic Matter (NBM) existed during this era, its influence on prehistoric society was purely environmental. Early clusters often settled near stable NBM pockets, likely drawn by the unique bioluminescence or the slight thermal anomalies created by high-concentration areas.

There is no archaeological evidence to suggest that NBM was utilized technologically during this period. To the First Ancestors, NBM was a part of the natural landscape—a mythic or spiritual presence that provided light and warmth, but remained beyond the reach of primitive manipulation.

Unlike the administrative Gravimetric Accounting of later eras, prehistoric interaction with NBM was likely limited to mythic interpretation and primitive aesthetics. Fragmentary cave etchings suggest NBM was viewed as a part of the natural 'weaving' of the world—a force to be respected, but not yet something to be quantified or exploited.

End of the Prehistoric era

The conclusion of the Prehistoric Era was not marked by a sudden catastrophe, but by a fundamental shift in the societal relationship with Non-Baryonic Matter. As migratory clusters began to linger longer at high-concentration sites, the purely observational reverence of the ancestors evolved into a more active interest.

Archaeological strata from the late 9th millennium BCE show an increased density of ritualistic items and primitive instruments near NBM pockets. This period saw the transition from viewing NBM as a static environmental feature to recognizing it as a force that could, perhaps, be influenced or utilized.

This intensified interaction acted as the catalyst for the first sedentary shifts. Clusters that had once been entirely nomadic began to establish semi-permanent foundations around NBM "wells," trading mobility for the unique stability and resources these sites provided. This convergence of previously disparate groups around shared gravitational anomalies laid the socio-technical groundwork for the Age of Convergence.
